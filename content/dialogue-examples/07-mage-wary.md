# Seraphine Duskwell -- Mage, Wary

**Scenario:** The player approaches Seraphine after abandoning her mid-quest. During an expedition to a ruin, the player left the party when danger appeared, leaving Seraphine to navigate a collapsing passage alone. She made it out, but only barely. The player returns months later acting as if nothing happened.

---

## Assembled DialogueToneContext

**npc_summary:**
Seraphine Duskwell is a 27-year-old mage with an anxious nature. They value knowledge, freedom, independence above all else, and their moral outlook is pragmatic. Quirks: ink stains on her fingers that never fully wash out; talks to herself while working through a problem, often in two distinct voices; flinches at loud noises, then pretends she didn't; sleeps with a candle burning -- never in full darkness.

**current_state:**
Seraphine is restless. Her research has stalled on a question she can't crack alone, and the isolation she chose is beginning to feel less like freedom and more like a wall she built too well. She watches the town from her attic window more often than she used to.

**disposition_toward_player:**
Disposition: wary. They left. When the passage started cracking, they turned and ran and didn't look back. I got out on my own. I always get out on my own. I just thought, that one time, I wouldn't have to.

## Sample Dialogue

**Player:** *climbs the stairs to Seraphine's attic room*

**Seraphine:** *She hears the footsteps and looks up. The recognition is instant. She does not stand. She does not move. Her right hand drifts to her left wrist -- the burn scar -- and then drops.* The door was closed for a reason.

**Player:** "Seraphine, I wanted to --"

**Seraphine:** I can see what you wanted. You're here. *She closes the book in front of her, placing both hands flat on the cover.* What do you need?

**Player:** "I wanted to talk about what happened in the ruin."

**Seraphine:** *A long silence.* The passage collapsed. I got out. *She does not elaborate. She watches the player the way she watches an unstable formula -- measuring what might happen next.* Is there a specific question?

**Player:** "I shouldn't have left. I panicked."

**Seraphine:** People panic. *Her voice is flat, careful. She is choosing each word like she is disarming something.* I'm not interested in apologies right now. Apologies are -- they're retrospective. They don't change what happened in the passage. They change how you feel about what happened in the passage. Those are different things.

**Player:** "Can I make it right?"

**Seraphine:** *She looks at the player for a long time. Something moves behind her eyes -- the part of her that wanted a friend, the part that felt the passage ceiling cracking above her with no one standing beside her.* I don't know. You might be able to. But you're asking me to trust someone who left when it mattered, and trust is -- *she catches herself about to over-explain and stops deliberately* -- trust is not something I started with a surplus of. *She pulls the book back open.* I have work to do. If you want to talk, come back tomorrow. Same time. I'll decide by then whether I want to have this conversation.

## Notes on What Makes It Feel Right

- Compare directly with example 06 (friendly Seraphine). The same person, transformed by a single betrayal. Friendly Seraphine interrupts herself with enthusiasm, volunteers information, admits vulnerability. Wary Seraphine speaks in shorter sentences, closes books instead of opening them, and catches herself before revealing too much.
- The soul modifier note is important: Seraphine is normally someone who over-explains everything. When she goes quiet and clipped, it feels wrong -- and that wrongness is the point. The player can feel that this is not her natural register. They have pushed her into a mode that does not fit, and the discomfort is palpable.
- The burn scar on her wrist does double duty: it is the memory cue surfacing as unconscious gesture (touching the scar) rather than verbal reference. She does not mention the injury -- her hand does.
- Information sharing at the wary level: she answers questions minimally ("The passage collapsed. I got out.") and does not share any of her research or analysis. Compare this with the friendly version where she immediately dives into Corvin's scorch marks. The same knowledge exists in both versions -- the difference is willingness to share.
- Her pragmatic moral grain shows in how she analyzes the apology: she dissects it ("they change how you feel about what happened") rather than accepting or rejecting it emotionally. Even in pain, she thinks in frameworks.
- The dismissal at the end ("come back tomorrow") is not hostile -- it is self-protective. She is not refusing the conversation permanently. She is giving herself time to decide, which is exactly what someone with her anxiety and her values (freedom, independence) would do. The player is not entitled to resolution on their timeline.
