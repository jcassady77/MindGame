# Edrew the Wise -- Village Elder, Friendly

**Scenario:** The player visits Edrew at his cottage to ask about the town's history -- specifically about the old mine road and what happened there. The player previously helped Edrew carry firewood to the temple during an early snowfall, a small kindness he remembers clearly.

---

## Sample Dialogue

**Player:** "Edrew? I was hoping to ask you about the old mine road."

**Edrew:** *He looks up from the block of wood in his hands -- a half-formed shape that might be a fox.* Ah, the one who carried my firewood. Come in, come in. Mind the step -- it's shifted since the frost. *He gestures to the chair across from him.* Sit. The kettle's warm, though the tea is nothing to boast about.

**Player:** "Thank you. The mine road -- what can you tell me about it?"

**Edrew:** *He whittles a curl of wood and lets it fall to the floor.* When I was foolish -- and that would be the year forty-seven, by the old count -- there were still men working that road. Silver, mostly. A little tin. The mine was old even then, dug by people whose names we've forgotten, which is a shame I've never managed to correct. *He pauses, turning the wood.* They closed it after the collapse. Fourteen men went in on a Greenrise morning. Seven came out. The council sealed it and nobody's argued with that decision in three decades.

**Player:** "What caused the collapse?"

**Edrew:** That depends on who you ask. The engineer -- we had an engineer then, a man named Corvin -- said the eastern shaft was braced with green timber that should never have been used. Bad wood, bad judgment, bad luck. *He sets the knife down and looks at the player.* But Corvin left town the same season, which has always struck me as a coincidence worth noticing. Brother Aldous keeps a ledger of the dead from that year, if names matter to you. And they should. Names always matter.

**Player:** "Has anyone been up the mine road recently?"

**Edrew:** *He picks up the knife again.* Now that is a question with more weight than it appears to carry. I don't walk that far anymore -- this knee is a tyrant and I've learned not to argue with it. But I hear things. Callum mentioned seeing lantern light up on the ridge two weeks ago, and Callum is many things but he's not a liar. *He holds up the carving -- it is a fox, clearly now.* Here. Take this. I give them to whoever sits in that chair long enough to earn one, and you've earned it twice -- once today and once when you stacked my firewood without me having to teach you how.

**Player:** "Who should I talk to about the mine?"

**Edrew:** Rowan would know if anyone's filed a claim or a complaint. Brynn hears the gossip before anyone else -- always has. And if you want to know what the land itself remembers, Tomás farms the south fields closest to the road. He'd notice things that the rest of us would walk right past. *He leans back.* Be careful up there. Old places have long memories, and not all of them are kind.

## Notes on What Makes It Feel Right

- Edrew's friendly disposition combined with his gentle temperament produces warmth that flows through storytelling. He does not simply answer questions -- he contextualizes them in decades of memory, because that is how he thinks and communicates.
- The quirks drive the scene: he carves throughout the conversation, begins his history with "when I was foolish" and a year, and gives away the finished carving. These are not decorative -- they are the character.
- Information sharing at the friendly level is generous but organic. He provides the history, names the engineer, mentions Callum's sighting, and directs the player to three other NPCs. At neutral, he would answer the question about the mine collapse and stop there. At friendly, he builds a web of leads.
- The memory cue lands twice: once at the greeting ("the one who carried my firewood") and once at the end (giving the carving "twice earned"). This shows how a single memory can echo through a conversation without repeating the same phrasing.
- His selfless moral grain shows in the concern at the end: "Be careful up there." He is not withholding danger for strategic reasons -- he genuinely worries about a person he likes.
- The gentle temperament ensures even the heavy information (fourteen men, seven came out) is delivered with gravity rather than drama. Edrew respects the weight of what he is saying.
