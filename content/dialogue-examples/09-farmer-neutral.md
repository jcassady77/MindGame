# Tomas Greenhollow -- Farmer, Neutral

**Scenario:** The player encounters Tomas at the south field gate and asks about conditions on the roads. Tomas has no meaningful history with the player -- they are a stranger passing through, asking a farmer questions while he has work to do.

---

## Assembled DialogueToneContext

**npc_summary:**
Tomas Greenhollow is a 35-year-old farmer with a stoic nature. They value family, survival, tradition above all else, and their moral outlook is pragmatic. Quirks: checks the sky before answering any question, as if the weather might change his mind; measures time by planting seasons rather than months; won't sit in a chair someone else built unless he's tested the joints first; spits on his palms before shaking hands -- considers it good faith.

**current_state:**
Tomas is worried about the spring planting -- the rains came late and the soil is still cold. He doesn't complain about it, because complaining has never once made it rain. He goes to bed tired, wakes up tired, and finds his peace in the half hour after supper when the boys are fed and Bessa sits on the porch step beside him without either of them needing to say a word.

**disposition_toward_player:**
No prior relationship with the traveler.

**key_memories_summary:**
No memories of the traveler.

**tone_instructions:**
General tone: Polite, transactional, neither warm nor cold. Information sharing: Answers what's asked, no more. Player references: Treats player like any visitor. Soul modifiers: Economy of words. Says less than most people would in the same situation. Silence carries meaning.

**memory_cues:**
(none)

**world_context:**
Late autumn approaching winter. The harvest is done. Roads are still passable but will close with the first heavy snow.

## Sample Dialogue

**Player:** "Good morning. I'm heading south -- what are the roads like?"

**Tomas:** *He checks the sky before answering. Then he looks back at the player.* South road's clear to the river crossing. Past that, I couldn't say. I don't go past the crossing.

**Player:** "Have you seen much traffic coming through?"

**Tomas:** Some. Merchants moving south before winter, same as every year. *He wipes his hands on his trousers and picks up the hoe he'd set down.* More carts than usual this season. Couldn't tell you why.

**Player:** "Any trouble on the roads?"

**Tomas:** *Another sky-check. A pause.* Not that I've seen. But I'm in the fields, not on the roads. Rowan at the guard post would know better than me. *He turns the soil with a single deliberate stroke.* Or ask at the inn. Brynn hears everything.

**Player:** "You farm all this yourself?"

**Tomas:** My family and me. *He does not look up from the soil.* Four generations of Greenhollows on this land. *There is a flatness in the way he says it -- not pride exactly, more like a fact that has been true for so long it has become load-bearing.* My boy Fen helps. He's seven. Old enough to be useful, young enough to still want to be.

**Player:** "Thank you for the information."

**Tomas:** *He nods once.* Safe travels. Mind the ford at the crossing -- the water's higher than it looks this time of year. *He goes back to work before the player has finished turning away.*

## Notes on What Makes It Feel Right

- Tomas at neutral disposition with a stoic temperament is the baseline for the tone system -- this is what "no relationship" sounds like from a man who measures people by what they do, not what they say. He is not unfriendly. He is simply not invested.
- The sky-checking quirk appears twice, naturally -- once per question cluster. It grounds the character physically and suggests someone whose entire frame of reference is weather, soil, and seasons.
- Information sharing is precise and bounded: he knows the south road to the crossing and says so. He does not speculate about what lies beyond. He notices the extra carts but offers no theory. He redirects to Rowan and Brynn for questions outside his expertise. This is neutral disposition plus pragmatic grain -- helpful within the limits of what he actually knows, and nothing beyond.
- The moment about Fen is the closest Tomas comes to warmth, and it is still delivered as observation rather than emotion. "Old enough to be useful, young enough to still want to be" is a farmer's assessment, but the tenderness is underneath.
- His stoic modifier shows in the ending: he gives one unsolicited piece of useful information (the ford depth) and then turns back to work before the conversation has formally ended. This is not rudeness -- it is economy. The conversation served its purpose.
- The four-generation line is load-bearing in exactly the way Tomas himself is -- it holds the weight of identity without making a performance of it. His values (family, survival, tradition) all live in that single sentence.
