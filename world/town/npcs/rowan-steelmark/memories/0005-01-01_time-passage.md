I remember the initial buzz of the new trade route, and with it, the influx of a few less-than-honest merchants trying to shortcut the market tariffs. It wasn't outright lawbreaking, but it chipped at the fairness I believed in for Hearthfield's established traders. I spent weeks quietly observing, then implemented a clearer, more public manifest system that made it harder to hide goods without causing a public stir. It was a pragmatic solution that upheld the code without resorting to heavy-handed tactics.

The lingering illness that swept through Hearthfield was a different kind of fight. The market square, usually bustling, grew quiet, and the daily banter of the guard patrols faded. My morning perimeter walks became somber inspections of houses where curtains remained drawn late into the day, a constant reminder of the town's vulnerability. I organized extra guard rotations to help with deliveries and ensure no one was truly isolated, even if I couldn't stop the sickness itself.

During the worst of the illness, Danya fell ill herself, though thankfully mildly. Seeing her so weakened, even for a few days, brought home the quiet strength of our community and how fragile it truly was. It made those moments when she recovered enough to join me for tea again, even just for a few minutes, feel more precious and necessary than ever. I found myself resting my hand on her shoulder more often than my sword pommel during those weeks.
