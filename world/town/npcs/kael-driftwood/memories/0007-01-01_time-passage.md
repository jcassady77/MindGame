The winter was a clawed beast, a cold I felt down to my bones. Grain stores dwindled, and I saw the strain on every face, the same look of hungry desperation I knew from other places. Yet, they pulled together, sharing what little they had. It wasn't what I'd call comforting, but I recognized the efficiency of it; a united front against a common enemy, something I hadn't seen in a long, long time without a commander's whip.

The spring bloom and the harvest that followed were a relief I hadn't realized I was holding my breath for. The sight of full granaries and bustling markets eased a deep, old worry about scarcity. This town, battered but not broken, found its 'quiet, resilient hope,' and I found myself watching it, a silent, pragmatic acknowledgment that survival here was possible, even likely.
---
date: 0007-01-01
type: interaction
---
After the winter, Old Man Hemlock needed help clearing debris from his barn roof; I found myself there without much thought, just doing the work. He didn't offer coin, just a silent nod and later, a mug of warm spiced cider. It was a simple exchange, but it settled in me differently than before; less obligation, more... quiet acceptance of a shared burden, almost.
