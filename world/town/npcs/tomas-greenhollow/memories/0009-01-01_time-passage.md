I'd heard the talk of the new bridge for months, and usually, talk like that means more trouble than it’s worth. But when they finally finished it, spanning the Whisperwind, I saw the truth of it myself. The old route to market had always been a muddy struggle after a rain, costing us time and risked a broken axle. Now, my cart runs smooth and true, and what used to take half a day's extra effort now takes barely an hour. It’s a good, solid piece of work, and even if I don't trust grand plans, I can’t argue with less mud on my boots.

Fen, at eleven, is nearly as tall as Bessa now, and his back is getting stronger with every season. He can lift a full sack of grain without much trouble. Oster, seven, still chatters like a magpie, but he helps gather eggs without breaking too many, and sometimes even tries to mimic Fen in the fields. It’s a quiet satisfaction, seeing them grow healthy and strong without the constant gnawing worry of lean times. This prosperity means they eat well, and that's what matters.

There's a strange quiet about these years, not the tense quiet of waiting for bad news, but the hum of things simply working. The fields have yielded fair, the sheep are fat, and the roof I patched still holds strong. I found myself leaning on the fence line one evening, watching the last light fade, and realized there hasn't been a Greenhollow in generations who didn't face some struggle. It feels... unsettling, this peace, but I'll take it. This land, our land, it endures.
