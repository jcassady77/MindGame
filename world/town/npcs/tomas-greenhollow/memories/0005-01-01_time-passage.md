That first year, with the good harvest, I finally managed to patch the sag in the barn roof that had bothered me for seasons. Bought good, sound timber from old Man Thistle down the road, and spent three solid days up there, sweat stinging my eyes. The feeling of seeing it true and straight against the sky, knowing our winter stores would be safe from the damp, was a quiet satisfaction. A man’s work is his only promise.

Then came the cough. Oster, little as he is, seemed to catch it worst of all. Not enough to truly worry about his life, but enough to make him sluggish and pale for weeks. Bessa brewed her teas, and I kept a fire going day and night, checking his breathing in the dark. It drained us all, that slow, nagging sickness, a constant hum of worry beneath the surface of every day's work.

The town wasn't itself, not for a long while after the sickness settled in. The market was quieter, voices lower, and the usual bustle replaced by a weariness you could feel in the air. People relying on healers or talkers seemed to fare no better than those who just put their heads down and endured. It just proved what I already knew: when things turn sour, a man's best hope is his own two hands and the land beneath his feet.
