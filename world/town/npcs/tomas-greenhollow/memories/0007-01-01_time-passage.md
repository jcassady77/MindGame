The first year, that winter gnawed at the bones. Every morning, I'd stare at the meagre grain in the stores, calculating if it would last, if my boys would have enough. Bessa fretted, and I just worked harder, chopping wood till my hands bled, trying to keep the cold from creeping into our small cottage. It wasn't about hope then, just about putting one foot in front of the other.

---
But then the next spring came, and the land, stubborn as an old mule, gave back more than we had any right to expect. Fen, a strapping lad of eleven now, worked like a grown man beside me in the fields, his strength a blessing. We filled the barn, saw the grain barrels overflowing, and that heavy knot in my gut finally loosened. A good year can make you forget the bad, almost.

Oster, seven now, still prattles on, but even he understood the turn of the seasons. I remember him drawing the fat wheat stalks in the dirt with a stick, mimicking Fen and me. Watching him, and Bessa by the fire mending a shirt, I felt a quiet certainty that we’d make it, that the Greenhollows would see another winter through. The land keeps its promises if you keep yours.
