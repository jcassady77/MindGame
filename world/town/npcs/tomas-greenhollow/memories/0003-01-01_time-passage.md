The second autumn's harvest was a good one, better than I'd dared hope after the rough spring. Fen, now a sturdy nine, pulled his weight more than ever, his small hands calloused from the hoe. Watching him work alongside me, back bent like mine, a quiet pride settled in my chest, a feeling as solid as the earth beneath our feet. We stored enough to see us through the winter, and then some, easing the constant knot in my gut.

Oster, now five, is a whirlwind of questions and boundless energy, always underfoot but never truly in the way when the day's work is done. I remember one evening, Bessa humming by the fire while he tried to draw a chicken with a lump of charcoal, his tongue poking out in concentration. Those quiet moments, the sounds of my family breathing around me, are the only true rest a man gets.
